  • What is this?

  • Bridgy copies comments, likes, reshares, and event RSVPs from social networks to your web site. If you post links to your site on social networks (aka POSSEing to the silos), and people interact with those links, Bridgy backfeeds those interactions to your site. You can see an example here.

    The first version of Bridgy was released a couple years ago, but only supported backfeeding comments to WordPress. This version is better, thanks to webmentions and the inspiration of the IndieWeb community.

  • How does it work?

  • When you add one of your accounts, Bridgy checks it periodically for posts and responses to those posts. When it finds a new response, it looks for links to your web site in the post, then sends webmentions to those links. It also translates the comment to microformats2 HTML so that your web site can fetch and parse it.

    Bridgy is open source. It's written in Python and runs on Google App Engine. It uses oauth-dropins to let users connect to the silos, activitystreams-unofficial to fetch data from the silos and convert it to a standard format, and webmention-tools to send webmentions.

  • How much does it cost?

  • Nothing! I have a great day job, and Bridgy is small, so thanks to App Engine, it doesn't cost much to run. I don't need donations, promise.

    If you really want to contribute, jump into the code and send me a pull request, or feel free to donate to the ASPCA or the EFF in my name.

  • Which things does it send, specifically?

  • Bridgy detects and sends webmentions for:

    • Twitter @-replies, retweets, and favorites of your tweets.
    • Facebook comments and likes on your posts...but not reshares of them, unfortunately. I haven't yet found a way to get those from Facebook's API.
    • Facebook event RSVPs. Details.
    • Google+ comments, +1s, and reshares of your posts.
    • Instagram comments and likes on your pictures.

  • What do you do with my data?

  • Nothing! Bridgy isn't a business, and never will be, so I don't have the same motivations to abuse your data that other services might. More concretely, I won't ever send you email, I store as little of your PII (personally identifiable information) as possible, and I never have access to any of your passwords.

  • Who are you? Why did you make this?

  • I'm Ryan Barrett. I'm just a guy who likes the web and likes owning his own data.

  • How long has this been around?

  • I started working on Bridgy in the summer of 2011 and launched the first version on Jan. 8 2012, which never got much adoption because it was WordPress only and not very polished. I launched the current version with webmention support on Dec. 9 2013.

  • How big is this?

  • On March 11 2014, 3 months after its relaunch, Bridgy had:

    • 155 users: 72 Twitter, 35 Google+, 34 Facebook, 14 Instagram
    • Seen 56,142 total responses (comments, retweets, likes, RSVPS, etc.)
    • Sent 4,576 successful webmentions to 702 different posts on 39 domains
    • Inspected 50k (estimated) pages for webmention endpoints

  • Bridgy stopped working for me!

  • Does your Bridgy user page have a icon on it? If so, Bridgy has lost access to your account. This can happen when you change your password, when Bridgy's access expires or is revoked, or for other reasons.

    To fix this, just click on the icon on your user page.

  • Bridgy used to work with my Facebook account, but now it doesn't!

  • Facebook only lets apps access your account for two months at a time. That time period automatically extends when you interact with an app yourself, but Bridgy works in the background, without user interaction. So, you'll need to come back every two months and log into Facebook again to renew its access to your account.

    Don't worry! You don't need to remember to do this yourself. Bridgy will send you a notification on Facebook when you need to renew. Just click on the notification, and you'll be set for another two months.

    • Pulling back responses

  • Bridgy isn't seeing one of my comments or likes or reshares!

  • Bridgy doesn't work in realtime, it just checks your accounts for new activity periodically, every 5 to 20 minutes or so depending on the site. Twitter favorites and retweets used to be an exception, since Bridgy used Twitter's Streaming API for a while, but not any more.

  • ...but it's been a long time, and Bridgy still hasn't seen it

  • Did you write the original post yourself? Bridgy doesn't crawl other people's posts, even if they have links to your web site. It only sends back responses to your posts.

    Is the post fully public? And the response too? Bridgy only shows and sends webmentions for fully public posts and responses. If your default Facebook audience is "friends" or "friends of friends," or your Twitter account is protected, or your Google+ post wasn't sent to "public," Bridgy won't see it.

    Instagram is the one exception. The Instagram API doesn't expose whether an account is private, so Bridgy handles all Instagram photos and comments. If your Instagram account is private, don't sign it up for Bridgy!

  • Bridgy isn't seeing one of my Twitter replies!

  • Does that reply include an @-mention of the user it's replying to? Twitter's API doesn't have a way to get a tweet's replies, so Bridgy searches for @-mentions of the tweet's author to find its replies. That means it can't always find replies that omit the @-mention.

  • What are all these brid-gy.appspot.com links in the responses that Bridgy sends to my site?

  • Short answer: your server's webmention code is probably rendering the webmention source URL. It should instead use the source page's microformats2 u-url property. This is a common oversight, since they're usually the same, but Bridgy is a special snowflake. :P You might want to file a bug against your server, or even fix it yourself. Here's an example discussion.

    Longer answer: Webmentions don't include the actual content of the response, just a link to it. The recipient fetches that link and extracts the content and other data using microformats2. The social networks don't (yet) support microformats2, so Bridgy translates their data to microformats2 HTML and serves those translations as webmention targets so that recipients can fetch and parse them.

  • OK, fine. But why brid-gy.appspot.com instead of brid.gy?

  • It's an SSL thing. The brid.gy domain fully supports and uses SSL, but it uses SNI, a newer SSL feature that some older clients and libraries have trouble with. Modern browsers are generally fine, but when Bridgy sends a webmention, the server that receives it also needs to understand SNI, and some still don't. appspot.com's SSL doesn't use SNI, so we use it for webmention sources in order to be as compatible as possible.


    • Publishing

  • What do you mean, publishing?

  • People often post something on their web site, then post a link to it on Facebook or Twitter so that other people will see it. The IndieWeb community calls this POSSE. There are lots of different reasons to do this, but one of the biggest is owning your data. Bridgy lets you do this faster, more effectively, and even automatically!

    You can try it right now. Once you're signed up to publish, plug the URL for one of your posts into the text box on your Bridgy user page, then hit the Preview button. You'll see a preview of what your post would look like as a Facebook post or Twitter tweet. If you like it, click Send and Bridgy will post it for you.

    It's not just for posts or tweets, either! You can use Bridgy to publish Twitter @-replies, retweets, and favorites and Facebook comments, likes, and even event RSVPs!

  • How does it decide which parts of my web page to include?

  • Magic! Most major blog engines and CMSes are supported out of the box, no setup necessary. Bridgy looks for microformats in your HTML, first the microformats2 e-content class and then the legacy entry-content class. It also understands more advanced microformats2 classes like in-reply-to, u-like-of, and p-rsvp. Here's an example: 

    <div class="h-entry"><p class="e-content">
  Two naked tags walk into a bar. The bartender exclaims, "Hey, you can't come in here without microformats, this is a classy joint!"
</p></div>

  • Can I do this automatically, whenever I post something new on my web site?

  • Definitely! Just send a webmention. Your server may be sending them already! Webmentions are pretty new, but more and more web servers are adding support for them. If you're not sure whether your server can send them, feel free to ask on #indiewebcamp, or you can even write a script to send them yourself. It's just a single HTTP request!

    Bridgy has two webmention endpoints, brid.gy/publish/facebook and brid.gy/publish/twitter. You can use one or both, depending on which social network you want to publish to. If your server sends webmentions, just include one or both of these invisible links in your post to publish automatically! 

    <a href="http://brid.gy/publish/facebook"></a>
<a href="http://brid.gy/publish/twitter"></a>

  • How do I comment on a Facebook post or reply to a Twitter tweet from my web site?

  • Easy! Put the comment or reply in a new post on your web site, and include a link to the post or tweet you're replying to with class u-in-reply-to, as if you were publishing a normal IndieWeb reply. For example: 

    <div class="h-entry">
  <p class="e-content">@schnarfed Highly entertaining. Please subscribe me to your newsletter.</p>
  <a class="u-in-reply-to" href="https://twitter.com/schnarfed/status/448205453911015425"></a>
</div>
    The link doesn't need to have any visible text in it. However, if you're replying to a tweet, you do need to @-mention its author. Then publish your post via the Bridgy UI or a webmention like normal!

  • How do I like a Facebook post, favorite or retweet a tweet, or RSVP to an event from my web site?

  • Liking, RSVPing, favoriting, and retweeting are almost exactly the same as replying. The only difference is that you use u-like-of for a like or favorite, u-repost-of for a retweet, or p-rsvp for an RSVP. 

    <a class="u-like-of" href="https://www.facebook.com/snarfed.org/posts/10100971276366173"></a>

    <a class="u-repost-of" href="https://twitter.com/schnarfed/status/448205453911015425"></a>

    <data class="p-rsvp" value="yes">I'm in!</data>
<a class="u-in-reply-to" href="https://www.facebook.com/events/1409507205977978/"></a>

  • Why only Facebook and Twitter? Why not Google+ or Instagram?

  • Sadly, Google+'s API doesn't support posting or commenting, and Instagram's API requires manual approval for commenting and liking. Shameless plug: check out #OwnYourGram for an Instagram alternative!
